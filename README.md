# globish-script
<div class="ds-markdown" style="--ds-md-zoom: 1.143;"><h1><span>Globish Script: A Shared Visual Mother Tongue for Humanity</span></h1><h2><span>Technical Proposal &amp; Community Initiative </span></h2><hr><h2><span>Introduction: The River of Writing and the Trend of Evolution</span></h2><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"></p><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>Since the dawn of human civilization, writing has carried thoughts, recorded history, and connected the world. From the cuneiform pressed into clay tablets by the Sumerians, to the hieroglyphs painted on papyrus by the ancient Egyptians; from the oracle bone inscriptions and bronze scripts of the Shang and Zhou dynasties, to the intricate symbols of the Mesoamerican Maya—every original civilization created its own pictographic script at its inception. These scripts are like mirrors, reflecting our ancestors' observations and expressions of the world.</span></p><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>Yet, the wheel of history never stops. With trade, cultural exchange, and imperial expansion, writing has continuously reinvented itself. The Phoenicians, as maritime merchants of the Mediterranean, pioneered the creation of 22 consonantal letters to record the speech of different languages. This innovation spread like wildfire to Greece, evolving into the Greek alphabet, which included vowels; it then gave rise to the Latin and Cyrillic scripts, covering all of Europe. To the east, the Aramaic script evolved into the Arabic and Brahmic scripts, influencing vast territories from North Africa to Southeast Asia. This clear evolutionary path reveals an iron law: </span><strong><span>the progression from complex pictograms to simplified phonetic systems, from the privilege of a few elites to a tool for the masses, is an irreversible trend in human civilization.</span></strong></p><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>Simultaneously, in East Asia, Chinese characters, with their unique logographic nature, have maintained cultural unity for millennia. Transcending phonetic drift, they allow people from different dialect regions to communicate through a single set of symbols. The stability and timelessness of Chinese characters prove that writing must carry cultural weight beyond mere sound. However, their complex strokes and vast number of characters make them difficult to master quickly on a global scale.</span></p><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>Entering the 21st century, the wave of globalization is sweeping the world at an unprecedented pace. Cross-border communication, information explosion, and the rise of artificial intelligence have made the need for a universal script more urgent than ever. We need a script that is as simple to learn as the Phoenician alphabet, yet as stable and meaningful as Chinese characters; one that can accurately record the pronunciation of every language while transcending dialects and the passage of time; one that aligns with the physiological habits of handwriting and adapts to the input and output of the digital age. Such a script could become humanity's shared </span><strong><span>"second mother tongue."</span></strong></p><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><strong><span>Globish Script</span></strong><span> is an attempt to answer this call of our era.</span></p><h2><span>Part I: Core Features of an Ideal Script</span></h2><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>Based on deep insights into the history of writing and a precise understanding of future needs, we propose </span><strong><span>seven core features</span></strong><span> of an ideal script, which serve as the design principles for Globish Script.</span></p><h3><span>1. Phonetic Foundation – Returning to the Essence of Speech</span></h3><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>Speech is the primary form of language; writing is a symbol system for recording it. Phonetic scripts use a limited set of symbols to spell an infinite number of words, with a much lower learning cost than logographic systems. The International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) represents the pinnacle of human phonetic science, accurately describing the sounds of all languages with the principle of "one sound, one symbol." Globish Script is benchmarked against the IPA, extracting </span><strong><span>45 core phonemes (30 consonants + 15 vowels)</span></strong><span> that cover the world's major languages. Each symbol corresponds to a </span><strong><span>unified, fixed phonetic value</span></strong><span>, truly realizing "what I write is what I speak, and what I speak is my sound."</span></p><h3><span>2. Learnability – A Gift of Systematic Design</span></h3><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>The human brain excels at recognizing patterns and rules. Globish Script groups consonants by </span><strong><span>place of articulation</span></strong><span>:</span></p><ul><li><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><strong><span>Bilabials</span></strong><span> are based on horizontal lines</span></p></li><li><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><strong><span>Alveolars</span></strong><span> are based on V-shapes</span></p></li><li><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><strong><span>Velars</span></strong><span> are based on sharp angles</span></p></li><li><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><strong><span>Glottals</span></strong><span> are based on curves</span></p></li></ul><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>Within each group, </span><strong><span>voiceless vs. voiced</span></strong><span> are distinguished by the </span><strong><span>presence or absence of a small loop</span></strong><span>, and </span><strong><span>aspiration or nasality</span></strong><span> are indicated by </span><strong><span>modifications at the endpoints</span></strong><span>. Vowels are formed by combining </span><strong><span>five basic directions (horizontal, diagonal, vertical) with three curvatures (straight, convex upward, convex downward)</span></strong><span> into 15 symbols – direction suggests tongue front/back position, while curvature suggests tongue height.</span></p><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>This highly systematic design means that once learners grasp the underlying patterns, they can derive all the symbols without rote memorization. </span><strong><span>Learn the rules, and you've learned all 45 symbols.</span></strong></p><h3><span>3. Readability – Visual Efficiency Through 2D Blocks</span></h3><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>When reading, the human eye recognizes whole shapes through saccades, rather than analyzing stroke by stroke. The brilliance of Hangul lies in combining letters into square blocks, condensing each syllable into a single visual unit, significantly boosting reading speed.</span></p><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>Globish Script inherits this wisdom, arranging the symbols of each syllable into a </span><strong><span>grid</span></strong><span>, forming a neat </span><strong><span>syllable block</span></strong><span>:</span></p><ul><li><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>1 symbol: centered</span></p></li><li><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>2 symbols: side-by-side</span></p></li><li><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>3 symbols: one above, two below</span></p></li><li><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>4 symbols: two rows, two columns</span></p></li><li><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>5 symbols: two above, three below (or vice versa)</span></p></li><li><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>6 symbols: three rows, two columns (or two rows, three columns)</span></p></li></ul><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>Regardless of how many symbols a syllable contains, each occupies its own distinct cell, providing clear visual boundaries for quick scanning. The curvature of vowels, the loops and hooks of consonants create strong shape contrasts within the block, allowing for fast differentiation even at small sizes.</span></p><h3><span>4. Writability – The Ultimate Simplification of One-Stroke Symbols</span></h3><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>Handwriting is humanity's most ancient mode of expression and a cornerstone of a script's practicality. The history of shorthand proves that writing speed is inversely proportional to the number of strokes.</span></p><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>Every symbol in Globish Script consists of </span><strong><span>a single, continuous curve</span></strong><span> – a straight line, a broken line, a line with a small loop, or a line with a hook. The average stroke count is </span><strong><span>less than 1.2</span></strong><span>, far fewer than the 2-3 strokes of Latin letters or the 7-8 strokes of Chinese characters. More importantly, all symbols can be written </span><strong><span>in one continuous motion without lifting the pen</span></strong><span>, aligning perfectly with the physiological instinct of handwriting.</span></p><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>Voicing distinctions no longer rely on variable pen pressure but are clearly marked by the </span><strong><span>presence or absence of a small loop</span></strong><span>, completely eliminating ambiguity caused by individual handwriting styles.</span></p><h3><span>5. Standardization – Millennial Stability Like Classical Arabic</span></h3><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>Classical Arabic, frozen by the sanctity of the Quran, has changed minimally for over a thousand years, serving as a timeless academic language. One of Globish Script's core goals is to anchor a </span><strong><span>standard set of phonetic symbols</span></strong><span> through ratification by the International Organization for Standardization (ISO).</span></p><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>Individual countries can establish their own </span><strong><span>orthographies</span></strong><span> (norms for spelling their language using this script), but the symbol system itself, once established, will not be easily altered by phonetic drift. Legal documents, scientific works, and historical classics can be passed down in a fixed visual form, achieving true </span><strong><span>"universal written language."</span></strong></p><h3><span>6. Two-Dimensionality – The Wisdom of Spatial Layout</span></h3><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>Linear scripts (like the Latin alphabet) consume significant horizontal space when writing long texts and make word boundaries harder to spot quickly. 2D layouts (like Hangul or Hanzi) can pack more information into the same area, and block boundaries naturally segment words.</span></p><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>Globish Script's grid layout rules </span><strong><span>dynamically adjust the number of rows and columns</span></strong><span> based on the total symbol count within a syllable. This adaptive approach maintains block regularity while avoiding wasted space from a fixed grid. It allows writers to intuitively produce well-proportioned blocks even on blank, unlined paper.</span></p><h3><span>7. Scalability and Practicality – A Future-Ready Design</span></h3><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>Language is alive; new words and new sounds constantly emerge. While Globish Script's 45 basic symbols cover the major phonemes, it reserves the capacity for expansion through </span><strong><span>combinations or diacritics</span></strong><span>.</span></p><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><strong><span>The Semantic Marker Block System:</span></strong><span> Six high-frequency symbols are selected from the 45. When placed after a </span><strong><span>separator (a special non-pronounced symbol)</span></strong><span> , they transform into </span><strong><span>non-pronounced semantic markers</span></strong><span>, used to distinguish homophones. For example:</span></p><ul><li><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>Mandarin Chinese "shìshí" (事实, fact) and "shǐshī" (史诗, epic) share the same pronunciation; they can be differentiated by adding the separator + different semantic markers after the syllable block.</span></p></li><li><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>English "write" and "right" can also be distinguished using semantic markers.</span></p></li></ul><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><span>The symbols after the separator are </span><strong><span>not pronounced; they only convey meaning</span></strong><span>, creating a hybrid "phono-semantic" script that combines the precision of a phonetic system with the clarity of a logographic one. This design enables Globish Script not only to record the sounds of any language but also to accurately convey meaning, paving the way for truly barrier-free global communication.</span></p><h2><span>Part II: Comparative Advantages Over Existing Systems</span></h2><div class="ds-scroll-area _1210dd7"><div class="ds-scroll-area__gutters" style="position: sticky; Questions</span></h2><h3><span>Q1: Can 45 symbols really cover all languages?</span></h3><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><strong><span>A</span></strong><span>: These 45 symbols cover over 90% of the common phonemes found in the world's major languages. Rare sounds can be represented through symbol combinations (e.g., using two symbols for one sound) or diacritics. The coverage will be confirmed by a team of linguists during Phase 1.</span></p><h3><span>Q2: If the phonetic values are unified, won't speakers of different languages misunderstand each other?</span></h3><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><strong><span>A</span></strong><span>: No. The phonetic variation of the same phoneme across languages is systematic (e.g., English /p/ is aspirated, French /p/ is unaspirated, but both are voiceless bilabial stops). When native speakers hear an approximate sound, they automatically map it to their own phoneme inventory and understand it with the help of context. It's like a Chinese person speaking English with an accent – an English speaker still understands.</span></p><h3><span>Q3: Won't the semantic marker system make the script too complex?</span></h3><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><strong><span>A</span></strong><span>: Semantic markers are optional, used only when needed to disambiguate homophones. High-frequency words use the simplest markers, and low-frequency words might not use markers at all, relying on context. The markers themselves are borrowed from the 45 symbols, requiring no new shapes to memorize. This adds a powerful "semantic tag" to the script with minimal overhead.</span></p><h3><span>Q4: How can Globish Script gain widespread acceptance?</span></h3><p class="ds-markdown-paragraph"><strong><span>A</span></strong><span>: We don't aim to "replace" existing scripts, but to exist as a </span><strong><span>supplementary tool</span></strong><span> – for language learning, cross-lingual dictionaries, international communication aids, etc. As digital tools proliferate and content accumulates, it will naturally permeate the contexts where it's needed.
